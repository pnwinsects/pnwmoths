## Identification



##### Adults

*Sympistis viriditincta* is a small (FW length 11 - 13 mm) brown moth with a pure white-filled oval orbicular spot and white peripheral filling of the other spots that has been reported from eastern British Columbia and flies in the fall. The forewing color is slightly darker and grayer on the distal wing. A white spot is located at the wing base, and a series of pale spots are present on the distal half of the costa. The lines are gray, slightly darker than the ground color. The antemedial line is slightly irregular and oriented slightly oblique from anteromedial to posterolateral. The median line is thicker than the other lines, scalloped, and variable from faint to dark. The postmedial line is moderately scalloped, excurved around the end of the cell and then slightly oblique toward the wing base below the cell. The subterminal line is made of individual white scales in an irregular line, preceded by a gray spot on the costa. The terminal line is a series of gray spots. The fringe is brown, weakly checkered with gray. The spots are outlined in gray with inner white line. The oval orbicular spot is filled with solid white. The kidney-shaped reniform spot and the small claviform spot are filled centrally with the ground color. The hindwing is grayish tan with gray median and postmedial lines, small discal spot, and poorly defined marginal band. The fringe is tricolored, yellow, gray, and white. The head and collar are brown. The rest of the thorax is brown sprinkled with white. The male antenna is filiform.

This species can be easily identified by the small size, brown color, and white spot basal spot and filling of the orbicular spot.

## Distribution



### Pacific Northwest

This species is reported from eastern British Columbia in Forbes (1954). No specimens from our area have been examined by us. The occurrence of this species in British Columbia needs to be verified.



### Global

This species is found in a narrow north-south distribution in southern Canada and the northern United States from Alberta through the upper Midwest to upstate New York.

##### Adults

Adults fly in the fall, reportedly in September and October (Forbes 1954). It is nocturnal and comes to light.
